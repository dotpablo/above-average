Four years ago I wrote something about the philosophy of living professionally ["always ready to quit."](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/activity-6813517266525671424-aTWK)

I didn't mean throwing in the towel — I meant operating without the mental lifejacket of "stability." At the time it seemed exaggerated. Today, for me, it's the only survival manual.

The general mood in the Tech environment (and in many corporations) you could cut with a knife. Mass restructurings, layoffs that arrive by email or Townhall, and that constant feeling of a hot seat.

But the reality check doesn't come from LinkedIn, nor from the number of people you see with the green "Open To Work" ring on their profile photo. It comes on Sunday, at the barbecue, when a relative comes up to you and whispers: *"Hey... I saw on the news that thousands were laid off in Tech. Are you okay? Do you still have a job?"*

That's when you realize. If your circle — which understands nothing about Tech, SAP, or APIs — can smell the fear, it's because the fire is real and it's not fake news.

> The person still betting on the "pseudo-comfort zone," believing their seniority or their hard-earned position means they're set — is literally in the oven.

---

### Confirmation from Those Who Know (It's Not Hype)

And if your aunt doesn't convince you, listen to those who move the pieces. What we're living isn't a coincidence:

If the people who own the board tell you the rules changed, stopping playing by the old rules isn't an option — it's an obligation.

Add the global crisis and inflation, and you have the perfect storm.

---

### The "Corporate Tricycle" Trap

Add to that complex equation of what could be "job stability" another danger coefficient: **the technological irrelevance you accumulate while you work.** I've seen this play out several times in different settings and the story never ends well.

There's a brutal disconnect today:
- **The Market:** Driving a Ferrari at 300 km/h using Frontier Models (Claude Opus 4.6, Codex, Gemini Deep Thinking, and the Chinese models that occasionally show up to upend the board) — models that reason, code, and create at superhuman levels.
- **The Corp:** Making you pedal a tricycle with a compliance-neutered Copilot, proxy blocks, bureaucracy that takes three months to approve a $20 license, and a ban on connecting to any MCP.

If you get used to the corporate turtle's pace, when you have to enter the jungle of the real market, you'll get eaten alive. And this isn't a threat — it's a spoiler.

### The End of the "Syntax Error" (And the Birth of the Orchestrator)

Last week I shared an idea: **the syntax error is dead.**

Writing code that compiles today is trivial thanks to AI. But this convenience comes with a hidden cost:

> **We traded visible syntax errors for invisible structural errors.**

This created a fork in talent:

Those who use AI to generate volume — but can't audit what it produced.

And those who use AI as a cognitive exoskeleton — who keep judgment, verify the output, understand the architecture.

I was listening to a podcast between Freddy Vega and Guillermo Rauch and they confirmed this. Rauch was surgical: the future of programming moves away from narrow technical specialization and toward a higher level of abstraction.

We're no longer "code typists" — we become (those who choose that path) **Agent Orchestrators**.

The podcast talked about returning to the era of the **polymath**, like Da Vinci. Where the value isn't memorizing syntax (which AI does better) but having:
- **Good Taste:** To distinguish an elegant solution from a patch.
- **Curiosity:** To connect dots AI doesn't see.
- **Integrity:** To take responsibility for the final result. That sense captured by the English word *Agency* (or as we say around here: **having the spine to own the outcome**).

### Human Skills (Your Life Insurance)

A lot of people see this and freeze. They see AI as the enemy. Let me make it short: **If you see AI as an enemy, you already lost that battle.**

Adapting today to this reality — where AI is the new way of working — doesn't require "soft skills" (that tepid HR term). It requires **Human Survival Skills**:
- **High Tolerance for Uncertainty:** Learning to live in an infinite loop where there's no explicit "end," just constant exceptions to manage without a manual.
- **Critical Thinking:** The ability to look at code AI produced and say *"this is garbage even though it compiles."*
- **Resilience:** The ability to reinvent yourself when the tool you used yesterday is obsolete today.
- **Eternal Student:** And I don't mean being the useless person who spends 15 years at university without graduating. I mean studying constantly. If you don't, you're digging your own grave and no superhero is going to save you.

### The Plan: Above Average (Get Out of Average Now)

Don't spend ammunition "defending your position." Your position isn't you. Your position is a temporary rental that depends on someone 3 or 4 levels above you in the org chart who answers to a Board.

Does the company not give you the tools? **Buy them yourself.** Invest in your own R&D. Stop buying a couple of Starbucks coffees and pay for the Claude/ChatGPT subscription that can literally change how you live.

**Are you afraid an agent will do your job?**

Have THREE agents do your operational work and use that freed-up time to think strategy and architecture.

If your boss sees that, you have two scenarios: either you get promoted or you get fired. **And if you get fired, they're doing you a favor** — because you were in the wrong place (and now you know how to defend yourself).

We're moving from selling our time to selling our judgment. AI can give you 100 solutions, but only you have the experience to know which of those 100 won't explode in six months.

**And if you don't have that experience yet?**

The only way to develop it is by breaking things. Judgment doesn't download from a prompt — it's earned by debugging your own errors and getting your hands dirty. Nobody debuts in the first division without having swallowed mud and wearing a few war scars.

There's a phrase in Uruguay/Argentina that I like, and it describes the sense of *Agency* well: **"hacete cargo"** — own it.

Update your *wetware* today.

The market waits for nobody (and nobody owes you anything).
