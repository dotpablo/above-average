The problem isn't time. It's the decision.

---

Last Monday I was talking with a colleague and mentioned I'd run my 19th marathon a month ago:

> "Pablo, it's easy for you. You probably don't have kids."

Max (4 years old) was literally sitting at my desk while I replied to that message.

Mía (almost 2) was crying with mom because she didn't want to get dressed for daycare.

I had a meeting with Germany in 15 minutes.

And I'd already had 2 hours of work done and had run the 12km on the training plan for that day…

How did I manage all that? I started before 5am.

Like always.

### The Statistic: The "I Don't Have Time" Lie

Average says they don't have time.

But the data says they have, on average, 5 hours and 40 minutes daily for this:
- **2h 23m on Social Media:** The DataReportal Digital 2024 report confirms it. Time that goes on reactive scrolling. Watching how other people live.
- **3h 17m on TV/Streaming:** Average time spent watching screens. Almost never learning something new.

Average has almost 6 hours of "dead time" per day.

> Average says "I don't have time" 12 times a week while looking at their phone.

Me:
- Max and Mía (4 and almost 2)
- Full-time work (leading global teams)
- 6 weekly training sessions (5-6am)
- 19 marathons completed
- 20+ books per year
- ~15 minutes of TV
- ~60 minutes of social media (some IG and LinkedIn)

Not because I'm special.

Because I eliminated **"I don't have time"** years ago.

---

### The Truth Nobody Tells You

"I don't have time" isn't a time problem.

It's a decision problem.

Every morning, Average wakes up and starts deciding:
- Do I get up or 5 more minutes?
- What clothes do I put on to train?
- Do I train today or tomorrow?
- What do I do first when I get to work?
- Do I answer this email or that one?

300+ decisions before noon.

Each decision consumes energy (it's called **decision fatigue**).

Each decision is an opportunity to procrastinate.

Average improvises every day.

**Above Average eliminated trivial decisions years ago (or starts today).**

---

### The System: How I Have Time for (Almost) Everything

**RULE #1: I DON'T MANAGE TIME. I ELIMINATE DECISIONS.**

Average: "Tomorrow I'll see if I train."

Me:
- Alarm at 4:50am (6 days a week, non-negotiable)
- Running clothes next to the bed (prepared the night before)
- Out of the house in less than 15 minutes
- Same route, follow the plan
- 0 decisions between the alarm and the first kilometer

Result: 1,000+ consecutive days training before 6:00 am.

Not because I have motivation.

Because there's no decision to make.

**RULE #2: FLEXIBLE ARCHITECTURE, NOT "LET'S SEE WHAT HAPPENS"**

My week isn't flexible. It's architecture.

This is the blueprint for my ideal day:
- **5:00-6:30am:** Training
- **7:00-8:00am:** Breakfast with Max and Mía (non-negotiable)
- **8:00-12:00pm:** Work I (pending objectives + meetings)
- **12:00-1:00pm:** Lunch
- **1:00-3:00pm:** Work II (light, new things)
- **3:00-5:00pm:** Deep Work (do not disturb)
- **5:00-6:00pm:** Above Average (this project)
- **6:00-9:00pm:** Family (non-negotiable)
- **9:00-9:30pm:** Reading (10-15 pages minimum)
- **9:30pm:** Sleep

Sounds utopian. And sometimes it is.

As the great Mike Tyson said:

> **"Everyone has a plan until they get punched in the face."**

99% of the time the plan fails because of things we can't control.

Average uses the first punch as an excuse to abandon the day.

*Above Average* knows the architecture isn't concrete. **It's Legos.**

A recurring example: kids got sick, bad night.

Average says: "Didn't sleep, won't train. The day is ruined."

I prefer to eat quickly in front of the computer than arrive at night knowing I didn't honor my priority.

The trick is not losing your priorities while solving the unexpected.

**Intelligence is defined as the ability to face uncertainty.**

This is where we stand out — being resilient and executing the system as best we can.

**RULE #3: PRIORITY VS. URGENCY (THE DAILY BATTLE)**

Every morning before opening the inbox:

**I write 3 priorities:**
1.
2.
3.

> EVERYTHING else is noise.

Urgent emails, MS Teams, last-minute meetings, "just 5 minutes."

I know, it sounds utopian, but you have to stick to that as much as possible for it to succeed and for your time to be respected.

Average opens the inbox and reacts.

I open the inbox after completing priority #1.

Result: 90% of my days I complete the 3 priorities before dark.

Average ends the day without having done what was important.

**RULE #4: BATCHING (GROUPING = EFFICIENCY)**

*Average:*
- Checks email 30+ times a day
- Switches tasks ("context switching") every 10 minutes
- Believes they're "multitasking" (spoiler: it doesn't exist)

The science is clear: "Context switching" destroys your productivity. American Psychological Association research shows it can cost **up to 40% of your productive time.** It costs energy and time (up to 23 minutes) to refocus.

Me:
- **Email:** 2-3 times a day (e.g., 11am and 4pm). 30-min blocks. The rest of the time, the tab is CLOSED.
- **Meetings:** Grouped. I try to have them only on Tuesdays and Thursdays. I say "no" to ones without an agenda or where I don't add value.
- **Deep Work:** 90-120 minute blocks. No phone. No Teams/Slack notifications.

It's not magic. It's design. It's avoiding the cost of the switch.

### The Secret Nobody Mentions

Average looks for "balance."

I don't look for balance. I look for seasons.
- **January-March:** Marathon season (minimum training volume)
- **April-June:** Recovery + family (medium volume, focus on home)
- **July-September:** Build-up (maximum volume, focus on work and maybe another marathon)
- **October-December:** Peak + work projects (controlled volume, focus on closing the year)

I can't be 100% in everything, all the time.

But I can be 100% in 1-2 things per season.

Average tries to do everything, all the time. Ends up doing nothing well.

---

**What doesn't work (and why I learned it)**
- "I'll get up early tomorrow" → Failed a thousand times until I eliminated the decision.
- "I'll train when I have time" → There's never time. There are priorities.
- "I need to be motivated" → Motivation lasts 3 days. Systems last years.
- "I'll do inbox zero" → The inbox never ends. Priorities do.
- "I need more hours in the day" → You don't need more time. You need fewer decisions.

---

### The Action (for this week)

Don't try to copy my complete system — each life and routine is different.

But you can start with this TODAY:

**STEP 1: ELIMINATE ONE DAILY DECISION**

**Choose ONE thing you want to do consistently:**

**If you want to train:**
- Clothes ready the night before (next to the bed).
- Alarm far from reach.
- Sneakers at the door.

**If you want to read:**
- Book on the nightstand (open to the current page).
- If the book gets dense, find another — no prizes for finishing something you don't enjoy.
- Phone in another room.
- 10 pages before closing your eyes.

**If you want to work better:**
- Write 3 priorities on paper before opening the laptop.
- Block (if possible) the first 2 hours of the day (no meetings, no messages) — or mid-shift.
- Inbox only after priority #1 is completed.

**STEP 2: MEASURE**

**Average assumes. Above Average measures.**

This week:
- How many days did you execute ONE thing without deciding?
- How many times did you say "I don't have time" (and what were you doing)?
- How many hours did you waste on scroll/TV? (Do the real math.)

You can't improve what you don't measure. It's impossible.
